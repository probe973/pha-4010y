Workshop: Concentrations - Answers and Solutions

Conversion between concentrations

  1. Convert the following concentrations into $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    a. 100 mg in 5 ml: $100~\text{mg} / 5~\text{ml} = 20~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    b. 1000 mg in 20 ml: $1000~\text{mg} / 20~\text{ml} = 50~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    c. 1 g in 100 ml: $1~\text{g} = 1000~\text{mg}$, so $1000~\text{mg} / 100~\text{ml} = 10~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    d. 1 g in 200 ml: $1~\text{g} = 1000~\text{mg}$, so $1000~\text{mg} / 200~\text{ml} = 5~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    e. 2 g in 500 ml: $2~\text{g} = 2000~\text{mg}$, so $2000~\text{mg} / 500~\text{ml} = 4~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    f. 10 g in 1 l: $10~\text{g} = 10000~\text{mg}$, $1~\text{l} = 1000~\text{ml}$, so $10000~\text{mg} / 1000~\text{ml} = 10~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    Which of the concentrations in question 1 is the weakest and which is the strongest?

    Weakest: 2 g in 500 ml (4 mg/ml)

    Strongest: 1000 mg in 20 ml (50 mg/ml)



  2. Convert the following concentrations into %w/v. %w/v means $\text{g}/100$ ml.

    $%\text{w}/\text{v}=\frac{\text{mass}(\text{g})}{\text{volume}(\text{ml})}\times100$

    a. 1 g in 100 ml: $\frac{1~\text{g}}{100~\text{ml}}\times100 = 1\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    b. 5 g in 100 ml: $\frac{5~\text{g}}{100~\text{ml}}\times100 = 5\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    c. 5 g in 1000 ml: $\frac{5~\text{g}}{1000~\text{ml}}\times100 = 0.5\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    d. 5 g in 50 ml: $\frac{5~\text{g}}{50~\text{ml}}\times100 = 10\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    e. 500 mg in 10 ml: $500~\text{mg} = 0.5~\text{g}$, so $\frac{0.5~\text{g}}{10~\text{ml}}\times100 = 5\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    f. 150 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$: $150~\text{mg}/\text{ml} = 15~\text{g}/100~\text{ml}$, so $15\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    Which two concentrations in question 2 have the same strength?

    5 g in 100 ml (5% w/v) and 500 mg in 10 ml (5% w/v)



  3. Convert each of the following concentrations into the given unit.

    a. 250 $\text{mg}/10$ ml into $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$: $250~\text{mg}/10~\text{ml} = 25~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    b. 250 $\text{mg}/10$ ml into %w/v: $250~\text{mg} = 0.25~\text{g}$, so $\frac{0.25~\text{g}}{10~\text{ml}}\times100 = 2.5\%\text{w}/\text{v}$

    c. 40% $\text{w}/\text{v}$ into $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$: $40\%\text{w}/\text{v} = 40~\text{g}/100~\text{ml} = 40000~\text{mg}/100~\text{ml} = 400~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    d. 2% $\text{w}/\text{v}$ into $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$: $2\%\text{w}/\text{v} = 2~\text{g}/100~\text{ml} = 2000~\text{mg}/100~\text{ml} = 20~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$



  4. Convert each of the following concentrations into %v/v.

    %v/v can be thought of as $\text{ml}/100$ ml (or $\text{l}/100$ l).

    a. 50 ml in 200 ml: $\frac{50~\text{ml}}{200~\text{ml}}\times100 = 25\%\text{v}/\text{v}$

    b. 2 ml in 50 ml: $\frac{2~\text{ml}}{50~\text{ml}}\times100 = 4\%\text{v}/\text{v}$

    c. 500 ml in 2 l: $2~\text{l} = 2000~\text{ml}$, so $\frac{500~\text{ml}}{2000~\text{ml}}\times100 = 25\%\text{v}/\text{v}$

    d. 0.5 ml in 500 ml: $\frac{0.5~\text{ml}}{500~\text{ml}}\times100 = 0.1\%\text{v}/\text{v}$



Ordering concentrations by strength

  1. Order the following concentrations from weakest to strongest.

    A: 50 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    B: 100 $\text{mg}/10$ ml = 10 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    C: 250 $\text{mg}/100$ ml = 2.5 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    D: 1 $\text{g}/500$ ml = 1000 $\text{mg}/500$ ml = 2 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    Order: D (2 mg/ml), C (2.5 mg/ml), B (10 mg/ml), A (50 mg/ml)



  2. Order the following concentrations from weakest to strongest.

    A: 5% $\text{w}/\text{v}$

    B: 2.5 $\text{g}/100$ ml = 2.5% $\text{w}/\text{v}$

    C: 5 $\text{g}/10$ ml = 50 $\text{g}/100$ ml = 50% $\text{w}/\text{v}$

    D: $0.8~\text{g}/\text{ml}$ = 80 $\text{g}/100$ ml = 80% $\text{w}/\text{v}$

    Order: B (2.5% w/v), A (5% w/v), C (50% w/v), D (80% w/v)



  3. Order the following concentrations from weakest to strongest.

    A: 1% $\text{w}/\text{v}$ = 1 $\text{g}/100$ ml = 10 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    B: $100~\text{mcg}/\text{ml}$ = 0.1 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    C: 1 $\text{mg}/5~\text{ml}$ = 0.2 $\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    D: $1~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    Order: B (0.1 mg/ml), C (0.2 mg/ml), D (1 mg/ml), A (10 mg/ml)



Calculating dosage

  1. A patient requires a drug dose of 200 mg. The drug comes in liquid form with a strength of $5~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$. How many ml does the patient need?

    Required volume = $\frac{200~\text{mg}}{5~\text{mg}}\times1~\text{ml} = 40~\text{ml}$



  2. A patient requires a drug dose of 500 mg. The drug comes in liquid form with a strength of 2% $\text{w}/\text{v}$ How many ml does the patient need?

    2% $\text{w}/\text{v} = 2~\text{g}/100~\text{ml} = 2000~\text{mg}/100~\text{ml} = 20~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    Required volume = $\frac{500~\text{mg}}{20~\text{mg}}\times1~\text{ml} = 25~\text{ml}$



  3. A patient requires a drug dose of 2 g. The drug comes in liquid form with a strength of $800~\text{mg}/10$ ml. How many ml does the patient need?

    2 g = 2000 mg

    Strength = $800~\text{mg}/10~\text{ml} = 80~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    Required volume = $\frac{2000~\text{mg}}{80~\text{mg}}\times1~\text{ml} = 25~\text{ml}$



  4. A patient is given a drug that comes in a strength of 0.4% $\text{w}/\text{v}.$ The patient needs 20 mg of the drug, twice daily for 7 days. What is the total number of ml required for the treatment?

    0.4% $\text{w}/\text{v} = 0.4~\text{g}/100~\text{ml} = 400~\text{mg}/100~\text{ml} = 4~\text{mg}/\text{ml}$

    Total dose needed = $20~\text{mg} \times 2 (\text{times per day}) \times 7 (\text{days}) = 280~\text{mg}$

    Total volume = $\frac{280~\text{mg}}{4~\text{mg}}\times1~\text{ml} = 70~\text{ml}$



  5. A particular drug comes in a strength of 10% $\text{v}/\text{v}.$ The patient needs an active dose of 5 ml per day for 5 days, followed by 10 days of 2.5 ml per day. What is the total ml needed to complete the treatment?

    Dose for first 5 days = $5~\text{ml}/\text{day} \times 5~\text{days} = 25~\text{ml}$

    Dose for next 10 days = $2.5~\text{ml}/\text{day} \times 10~\text{days} = 25~\text{ml}$

    Total active dose needed = $25~\text{ml} + 25~\text{ml} = 50~\text{ml}$

    Drug strength: 10% $\text{v}/\text{v}$ means 10 ml of active drug in 100 ml of solution.

    Total solution needed = $\frac{50~\text{ml} (\text{active dose})}{10~\text{ml} (\text{active in 100ml solution})}\times100~\text{ml} (\text{solution}) = 500~\text{ml}$
